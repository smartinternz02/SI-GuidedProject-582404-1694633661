visualizing the data

import seaborn as sns
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Import the car crash dataset using Seaborn
car_crashes_data = sns.load_dataset("car_crashes")

# Create a pairplot to visualize relationships among numeric columns
sns.pairplot(car_crashes_data)
plt.show()

# Generate a histogram for the total crash counts
sns.histplot(car_crashes_data["total"], bins=15, kde=True)
plt.xlabel("Total Crashes")
plt.ylabel("Frequency")
plt.title("Histogram: Total Crash Counts")
plt.show()

# Produce a scatter plot depicting the relationship between alcohol consumption and total crashes
sns.scatterplot(x="total", y="alcohol", data=car_crashes_data)
plt.xlabel("Total Crashes")
plt.ylabel("Alcohol Consumption")
plt.title("Scatter Plot: Alcohol vs. Total Crashes")
plt.show()

Inference is must for each and every graph

  1. Pairplot (Relationships between Numeric Columns):
    • The pairplot displays scatter plots for pairs of numeric columns and histograms for individual column distributions.
    • There is a strong positive correlation between "total" crashes and "alcohol" consumption, indicating that states with higher alcohol consumption tend to have more total crashes.
    • Additionally, there is a positive correlation between "speeding" and "not_distracted" variables and "total" crashes, suggesting that states with higher rates of speeding and fewer cases of distracted driving tend to have higher crash counts.
    • The histograms along the diagonal show right-skewed distributions for "total" crashes and "alcohol" consumption, indicating that most states have relatively lower values, with a few outliers having notably higher values.
  2. Histogram of Total Crashes:
    • The histogram visually represents the distribution of total crash counts across different states.
    • Most states have a peak in the 15-20 crash count range, suggesting that this range is common for total crashes.
    • Some states on the right side of the histogram have significantly higher crash counts compared to the majority, indicating that they are outliers in terms of crash frequency.
  3. Scatter Plot (Alcohol vs. Total Crashes):
    • The scatter plot explores the relationship between "alcohol" consumption and "total" crashes for each state.
    • While there is a positive trend suggesting that states with higher alcohol consumption tend to have more total crashes, it's crucial to remember that correlation does not imply causation. Other factors could contribute to this relationship.
    • Some states with low alcohol consumption still have high total crash counts, indicating that alcohol consumption alone is not the sole determinant of crash rates.
    • It's evident that multiple factors, such as road conditions, law enforcement, and demographics, likely influence crash rates in states. These factors need to be considered when analyzing and addressing road safety issues.
